The Sacred Arches: Tunisia's Mosque Heritage

Al-Masajid: The Soul of Tunisia

A study of light, geometry, and stone across thirteen centuries.

1. The Ifriqiyen Archetype (Aghlabid Style)

Founded in 670 AD, the Great Mosque of Kairouan served as the architectural model for all subsequent North African mosques.

The Square Minaret

The oldest surviving minaret in the world. Its massive, three-tiered square shape is inspired by Roman lighthouses and defense towers.

Hypostyle Prayer Hall

A forest of columns. It uses hundreds of antique marble and porphyry columns recycled from Roman and Byzantine sites (Sbeitla, Carthage).

2. The Zitouna: The Great Mosque of Tunis

Located in the heart of the Medina, it represents the evolution of the Fatimid and Ziride styles.

Architectural Highlights:

  • The Narthex Dome: A masterpiece of stone carving featuring intricate floral and geometric motifs.
  • The Horseshoe Arch: Unlike the Syrian round arch, the Tunisian arch is "broken" or slightly pinched, creating a more elegant silhouette.

3. The Ottoman Transformation (17th Century)

With the arrival of the Ottomans, the architecture shifted from horizontal and heavy to vertical and airy.

Feature Traditional Style (Maliki) Ottoman Style (Hanafi)
Minaret Shape Square (e.g., Kairouan, Zitouna) Octagonal (e.g., Hammouda Pacha)
Roofing Flat wooden ceilings Multiple Domes (Kouba)
Decor Carved Plaster (Naksh Hadida) Colorful Ceramic Tiles (Kallaline)

4. The Mihrab: The Focal Point

The niche indicating the direction of Mecca. In Tunisia, it is often a theater of luxury, decorated with white marble, zircon, and gold-leafed calligraphy.
